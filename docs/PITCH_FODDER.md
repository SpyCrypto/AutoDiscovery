## The Core Stats (Cited)

### 1 in 10 Federal Cases Has Discovery Problems Serious Enough for a Judge

> **~9.4–10.3%** of federal civil cases have a court ruling on a motion to compel.

- **Source**: Federal Judicial Center, *National Case-Based Civil Rules Survey* (2009). Table 1: "Rulings on motions to compel." Plaintiff attorneys reported courts granted motions to compel in 9.4% of cases; defense attorneys reported 10.3%.
- **Citation**: Emery G. Lee III & Thomas E. Willging, *Federal Judicial Center National, Case-Based Civil Rules Survey* (FJC 2009), available at [fjc.gov](https://www.fjc.gov/sites/default/files/materials/08/CivilRulesSurvey2009.pdf)
- **Marketing line**: *"Roughly 1 in 10 federal civil cases sees a motion-to-compel ruling — discovery problems serious enough to need the judge."*

### 230 Sanction Awards in 401 Federal Cases (Pre-2010 Study)

> In a comprehensive survey of federal eDiscovery sanctions opinions, courts issued **230 sanction awards** across **401 cases** where sanctions were sought.

- **Source**: Dan H. Willoughby Jr. et al., *Sanctions for E-Discovery Violations: By the Numbers*, 60 Duke Law Journal 789–864 (2010).
- **Citation**: [scholarship.law.duke.edu/dlj/vol60/iss3/7](https://scholarship.law.duke.edu/dlj/vol60/iss3/7)
- **Key breakdown from study**:
  - **354 of 401** sanction cases occurred after 2004 (88% in last 5 years of study)
  - **97 sanction cases in 2009 alone** — more than all years prior to 2005 combined
  - Defendants sanctioned **3x more often** than plaintiffs (175 vs. 53)
  - Most common misconduct: **failure to preserve** (90 cases)
  - Case types: employment (17%), contract (16%), IP (15.5%), tort (11%)

### Sanctions Granted Rates Over Time

> Spoliation sanctions granted rates have fluctuated dramatically — peaking at **63% in 2014**.

| Year | Sanctions Granted Rate | Source |
|------|----------------------|--------|
| 2007 | 23% | FJC Spoliation Study (2011) |
| 2010 | 60% | Gibson Dunn Annual Survey |
| 2011 | Peak volume: 150 cases | Gibson Dunn Annual Survey |
| 2014 | **63%** (peak grant rate) | Gibson Dunn Annual Survey |
| 2018 | 36% (post-2015 amendments) | Logikcull / Gibson Dunn |

- **Source**: Logikcull, *The Outsized Impact of Spoliation Sanctions*, citing Gibson Dunn year-end eDiscovery updates and FJC data. Available at [logikcull.com/learning/sanctions](https://www.logikcull.com/learning/sanctions/ediscovery-history-sanctions)
- **Note**: The 2015 FRCP Rule 37(e) amendments raised the bar for severe sanctions (now requires "intent to deprive"), which reduced grant rates. BUT this only applies to *federal* courts — **state courts like Idaho are not bound by the 2015 FRCP amendments** and may still impose severe sanctions more freely under state rules.

## 2024 Trends — It's Getting Worse, Not Better

From Kelly Twigger's *2024 eDiscovery Case Law Year in Review* (Minerva/eDiscovery Assistant):

- **6th consecutive year** with 200+ court rulings on mobile device discovery disputes
- Courts are **no longer accepting excuses** for lost text messages, WhatsApp data, or ephemeral messaging
- **Hyperlinked files** (cloud docs linked in emails) are now discoverable — new complexity
- **AI-generated content** is now subject to discovery — first wave of rulings in 2024
- **Legal hold notices** are losing privilege protection when spoliation is alleged
- Courts demand **hard numbers** for proportionality objections — "vague cost arguments are no longer enough"

- **Source**: Kelly Twigger, *2024 eDiscovery Case Law Trends: Sanctions, Strategy, and the Shifting Legal Landscape*, Minerva (2024). Available at [minerva26.com](https://minerva26.com/2024-ediscovery-case-law-trends-sanctions-strategy-and-the-shifting-legal-landscape/)
- **Marketing line**: *"Discovery is getting more complex every year — mobile devices, cloud storage, AI content. The risk of getting it wrong has never been higher."*

## Idaho-Specific Angles

- Idaho IRCP Rule 37 mirrors federal sanctions framework — **all the same risks apply**
- Idaho has **no mandatory initial disclosures** (unlike FRCP) — easier to miss deadlines when they're judge-ordered rather than automatic
- Idaho med-mal damages cap is **$509,013.28** — a single sanctions award could exceed the entire case value
- Idaho has **no limit on depositions** — open-ended discovery obligations = more room for error
